Streaming Video on Demand

HISTORY Vault · Lifetime Movie Club

Platforms iOS, Android, Web, Apple TV, Roku

Product Designer · HISTORY Vault: 1 year to launch across 5 platforms · Lifetime Movie Club: 6 months · Team: 4 designers, 6 engineers, 1 PM

The Problem

A+E had no subscription product

The business goal was to create HISTORY's first subscription video-on-demand product. There was no existing SVOD foundation to build on, no established patterns, and a hard commercial need to get to market.

The Constraint

Roku first, and fast

We needed HISTORY Vault on Roku quickly. So we gathered around a table and brainstormed what the OTT app should be and do. That speed requirement shaped everything: it meant designing for the most constrained platform first — a TV remote, a ten-foot interface with limited interaction.

Launch

Roku, then everywhere

HISTORY Vault launched on Roku, then rolled out across five platforms: Roku, Apple TV, iOS, Android, and Web. Each brought its own conventions at the time of this design. Each had to feel native to its platform while remaining recognizably one product.

Roku TV interface

iOS

From ten feet to ten inches

The Roku design gave us a structure simple enough to work with, the remote and a directional pad. Someone holding a phone on the other hand has a different context: they can touch, scroll, and search. Rather than shrinking the TV layout onto a phone, I rebuilt browsing so a viewer moving from their phone to their TV never had to relearn the product.

iOS app interface

Android

Native conventions, one product

Android users expected Android. At the time, I designed to Material Design conventions rather than mirroring the iOS app so the product felt native rather than transplanted. The harder line to hold was between platform-native and brand-consistent: the interaction patterns had to change, while the content hierarchy, structure, and identity stayed recognizably the same product across all five platforms.

Android app interface

Web

One product, every screen size

I designed the web experience to reflow around the content model rather than optimize for a single breakpoint: the same collections, the same hierarchy whether it opened on a thirteen-inch screen or a thirty-inch one. The product stayed recognizably itself across every device.

Responsive web interface for desktop and mobile browsers

Lifetime Movie Club

A second product, a different viewer

Within a year, Lifetime Movie Club launched a second SVOD product across the same five platforms, built on the shared foundation A+E had developed rather than from scratch. Same platform conventions, entirely different problem: where HISTORY Vault organized hundreds of hours of episodic documentary, Lifetime Movie Club surfaced a library of exclusive movies for a different audience with different browsing habits.

That contrast is where the design work went. My work went into what was genuinely different, the brand, the audience, and how a movie library wants to be browsed compared to a documentary archive.

Outcome

Two subscription products, each running across five platforms — ten platform launches in total. HISTORY Vault took a year to reach all five. Lifetime Movie Club took six months. The second product shipped in half the time because it didn't start from zero: the platform conventions, components, and patterns already existed. Both products are still live on all 5 platforms and are still being iterated on today.
